Modeling Wage Inequality and Endogenous Growth¶

Matt Daniels¶

April 16 2019¶

Can an endogenous growth model with O-Ring production account for rising wage inequality?¶

 

  • Here I will present a "simple" model in which monopolistically competitive firms invest in research to improve their productivity, similar to Jones and Kim (2014)
  • New technology is modeled as the result of O-ring cooperation among skilled innovators
  • Simulation results (VERY rough) suggest:
    • Model generates the familiar fat tail in firm productivity and wages
    • Skill complementarities can drive a modest increase in inequality for some range of increases in skill
    • But overall unlikely to explain the increase in top inequality

Motivation: Rising Inequality¶

Motivation: Pareto income (and wages)¶

  $$F(x)=1-(\frac{x_m}{x})^\alpha \quad for \quad x \in [x_m,\infty) \quad and \quad x_m,\alpha>0$$

  • Pareto (1896) noted that the top portion of of income distribution follows such a power law
  • At the top of the distribution, inequality is "fractal":
    • Ratio of top 1% to top 10% incomes is the same as that between top 0.1% and 1%
    • In recent years the Pareto parameter has been decreasing, yielding greater inequality
  • O-Ring type models are known to produce Pareto distributions

Motivation: Rising college wage premium¶

 

  • From 1979 to 2016 the percent of workers with a 4-year degree rose from 23 to 40 percent
  • Relative wage for college educated went from 134 percent to 168 percent
  • College educated workers disproportionately engage in knowledge work
    • And this work tends to emphasize teams and co-location
    • Geographically, skilled workers cluster and recieve higher wages
  • I model productivity gain as the output of knowledge work
    • Similar to much of the endogenous growth literature

Related literature: Endogenous growth¶

  • Beginning with Romer (1986) a class of models has viewed R&D as a deliberate process to develop technology and reap monopolistic profits from its use
  • Jones and Kim (2014) demonstrate that an endogenous growth model could generate a Pareto distribution of top incomes
    • Schumpeterian growth model in which entrepreneurial effort lowers the marginal cost of the entrepreneur's variety
    • Growth in productivity of the entrepreneur follows a geometric random walk
    • Also demonstrated that labor income (broadly defined) is a large part of the story of rising inequality in the data

Select process known to generate Pareto distributions¶

Benhabib and Bisin, 2017¶

  • Talent: An exponential distribution in talent $f_s(s)=pe^{-ps}$ combined with exponential returns to talent $y = e^{gs}$
  • Schooling: Marginal cost of schooling exponentially distributed then so is human capital, so earnings are Pareto
  • Span of control: Managers of skill s (uniformly distributed) hire agents to produce with function $f(n,s) = sn^\alpha$
  • Assortative matching: O-Ring theory of production due to Kremer (1993) has workers produce "multiplicatively", i.e.: $$E[Y]=k^\alpha(h_1h_2...h_m)mB$$ where $h_i$ is the human capital. Then a uniform distribution over human capital yields a truncated power law distribution (similar to Pareto)
  • Hierarchical production: Production organized into levels 1,2,...,I in which technology requires $n_i=\gamma n_{i+1}$

Model¶

  • Final good is produced via intermediate goods, that are themselves produced through monopolistic competition: $$Y=(\int_{0}^{1}Y_{i}^{\theta}di)^{\frac{1}{\theta}}$$ $$Y_{i}=x_{i}^{\alpha}L_{i}$$ where $x_i$ is the productivity of producer $i$ and $\alpha = (1-\theta)/\theta$
  • Firms improve their productivity according to: $$\dot{x_{i}}=An_{i}h_{i}^{n_{i}}x_{i}^{\phi}$$
    • $A$ is the productivity of researchers
    • $\phi$ represents the degree of "fishing out" in the production of technology
    • $h_i \in (0,1)$ is the level of human capital
    • $n_i$ is the number of workers engaged in improving productivity at firm i
  • Firms die with constant rate $\delta$

Model (cont'd)¶

  • Following derivation of Jones and Kim (2014), instantaneous profit in this model is: $$(1-\theta)X_{t}^{\alpha}(\frac{x_{it}}{X_{t}})L_{t}$$ where $X_{t}$ is the mean of firm productivities $x_{it}$
  • The wage of production workers is: $$w_t=\theta X_{t}^{\alpha}$$
  • Wages for skilled workers engaged in research is determined by firms marginal willingness to pay

(take everything with a generous helping of salt)¶

  • I've conducted very rough simulations to get a sense of how the model performs
  • Some shortcuts and computational issues, so this is highly preliminary
    • For instance firms choose team size to maximize growth (rather than profit)
